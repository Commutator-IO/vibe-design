# Shape a Pitch

Turn a raw idea into work that's **rough, solved, and bounded** — concrete enough to bet on, loose enough to leave the team room to solve. Shaping is closed-door work done before betting; keep it fast and low-fidelity.

## 1. Set the appetite
Decide how much time the idea is *worth*, not how long it would take. Two common appetites:
- **Small Batch** — a fraction of a cycle (one or two weeks of a team's time).
- **Big Batch** — a full six-week cycle.

The appetite is a constraint that shapes the solution. If a good solution won't fit the appetite, narrow the problem — don't inflate the time.

## 2. Narrow the problem
Respond to the raw idea by finding the specific problem worth solving. Watch out for **grab-bags** ("redo the calendar") — pin down the concrete use case and the actual pain behind it.

## 3. Find the elements (rough the solution)
Move at the right speed — sketch the solution's shape without designing screens. Two tools:
- **Breadboarding** — like a circuit diagram: list *places* (screens/pages), *affordances* (buttons, fields, actions) on each, and *connection lines* between them. No visual design, just flow and components.
- **Fat-marker sketches** — deliberately low-fidelity drawings (as if drawn with a thick marker) so no one over-invests in details the team should decide.

The **elements** are the output — enough to convey the solution, not a deliverable design.

## 4. De-risk: rabbit holes and no-gos
Before writing it up, hunt for anything that could blow the appetite:
- **Rabbit holes** — hidden complexity, unknowns, technical unknowns, interdependencies. Patch them: make a decision now, cut the tricky part, or spike it. Present to technical experts if unsure.
- **No-gos** — explicitly declare what's out of scope. Cutting back is how you make the work fit.

## 5. Write the pitch
Five ingredients:
1. **Problem** — the raw idea and the specific pain, ideally one concrete use case.
2. **Appetite** — how much time it's worth (Small/Big Batch).
3. **Solution** — the shaped elements; embed annotated breadboards / fat-marker sketches so they can *see* it.
4. **Rabbit holes** — details worth calling out to avoid trouble.
5. **No-gos** — what's explicitly excluded.

## Exit checklist
Appetite set · problem narrowed · solution breadboarded/sketched · rabbit holes patched · no-gos declared · pitch written with all five ingredients. Ready for `bet-the-cycle`.
