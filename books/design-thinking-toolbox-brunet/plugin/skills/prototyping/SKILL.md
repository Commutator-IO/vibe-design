# Prototyping & Experimentation (Deliver — test)

Make the shortlisted ideas **tangible as cheaply as possible** and put them in front of users to learn. The goal is learning, not polish — build only what a test needs.

## Choose the lightest prototype
Match fidelity to the question you're answering:
- **Dessin / bande dessinée** — sketch or comic-strip the experience or flow.
- **Prototype Quick and Dirty** — the fastest rough artifact that makes the idea real.
- **Pâte à modeler / argile / Lego®** — model physical products or systems with clay or bricks.
- **Prototype d'appli ou de site** — paper or clickable mock of a screen flow (no real code).
- **Jeu de rôle de prototypage** — act out a service interaction to feel where it breaks.
- **FabLab / atelier** — build a physical prototype when the idea is a product.

## Design the experiment
- **Service blueprint** — map the front-stage (what the user sees) and back-stage (what makes it work) of a service, including support processes.
- **Expérimentation pilote** — run a small, real-world pilot with limited users.
- **Turc mécanique (Wizard of Oz)** — fake the automated part with humans behind the scenes to test the concept before building it (a POC trick).

## Test with users
- **Interview-test** — sit a real user with the prototype: give a task, ask them to think aloud, don't lead, watch where they struggle. Capture reactions neutrally.

## Principles
- Prototypes are disposable and cheap — kill your darlings.
- One prototype per open question; test the riskiest assumption first.
- Show, don't tell — put it in the user's hands and observe.

## Decide
Map results back to the challenge and persona. **Iterate** (loop back to `analysis` or `ideation` if the frame or idea was wrong) or **advance** to `implementation`.

## Exit checklist
Right-fidelity prototypes built · experiment/test designed · tested with real users thinking aloud · feedback captured neutrally · iterate-or-advance decision made. Proceed to `implementation`.
